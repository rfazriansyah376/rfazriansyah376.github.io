Severity Assessment: The 4-Level Framework from Leaked Protocols

Not all style guide violations are created equal. An incorrect hashtag requires a different response than content that creates legal liability. The 4-level severity assessment framework revealed in leaked crisis response manuals provides clear criteria for determining violation seriousness and guiding appropriate responses. This framework prevents overreacting to minor issues while ensuring serious violations receive immediate, decisive action.

Level 1: Minor Violations are technical oversights that don't impact brand integrity or legal compliance. Examples include: using a slightly off-brand color shade, minor caption tone deviations, or formatting inconsistencies. These typically result from oversight rather than intention. According to leaked violation databases, Level 1 violations represent 62% of all incidents but cause only 8% of brand damage. The appropriate response is educational and corrective rather than punitive.

Level 2: Moderate Violations affect brand presentation or campaign effectiveness but don't create legal or reputational risk. Examples include: significant visual guideline deviations, major voice/tone misalignment, or failing to highlight key product features. These violations suggest either misunderstanding of guidelines or prioritization issues. They represent 28% of incidents and 22% of brand impact. Responses should be more formal with clear expectations for correction.

Level 3: Major Violations create actual or potential brand damage, though not immediate crisis. Examples include: incorrect product claims, inappropriate content associations, or significant disclosure issues. These violations require immediate correction and may involve contractual consequences. They represent 8% of incidents but 45% of brand impact. Responses must be swift and decisive.

Level 4: Critical Violations create immediate brand crisis, legal liability, or reputational damage. Examples include: illegal content, offensive material, false advertising claims, or association with prohibited entities. These are rare (2% of incidents) but cause 25% of brand damage. Responses require immediate content removal, executive involvement, and potential legal action.
