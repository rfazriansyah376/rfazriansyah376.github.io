Andorra's events calendar tells the story of a country that honors its past while embracing the present. The annual cycle of festivals and celebrations weaves together Romanesque traditions that predate the nation itself, modern music festivals that draw international performers, sporting events that test elite athletes against Pyrenean terrain, and village celebrations where ancient fires still burn in mountain squares. Timing a visit to coincide with one of these events transforms a standard holiday into something memorable. The festivals are not performances staged for tourists. They are genuine community celebrations that visitors are welcomed to witness and sometimes join. The music is live and often free. The atmosphere is authentic rather than manufactured. This guide maps the Andorran year through its events, helping you align your travel dates with the experiences that interest you most.

The Andorran festival calendar

The rhythm of Andorran life follows a calendar that blends the liturgical year of the Catholic Church with the agricultural cycle of the mountain valleys and the modern demands of tourism. The winter season centers on Christmas and New Year celebrations, followed by the ski season that draws visitors through April. Spring brings Easter observances, the blooming of the valleys, and the first outdoor festivals. Summer explodes with village festes majors, music festivals, and outdoor cultural events that take advantage of the long evenings and mild temperatures. Autumn winds down the festival calendar with harvest celebrations, mushroom fairs, and the chestnut festivals that mark the turning of the year. Each season offers distinct events, and a trip timed to coincide with a festival adds a dimension to the Andorran experience that no museum or monument can provide.

The events described in this guide are a selection of the most significant and visitor friendly celebrations. Many smaller events occur throughout the year, announced through parish tourist offices and local media. The spontaneity of discovering a village celebration not listed in any guidebook, a band playing in a square, a procession forming outside a church, a communal meal being laid out on trestle tables, is one of the pleasures of travel in Andorra. The major events provide anchors for planning. The minor events provide serendipity for those who remain open to it.

Winter events December to February

The Christmas season transforms Andorran towns with lights, markets, and celebrations that run from late November through early January. The Christmas Village in Canillo has grown into the country's signature winter event, drawing families from across Andorra and beyond. Wooden chalets sell crafts, local products, and seasonal treats. An ice skating rink provides entertainment for children and adults. The village operates throughout December and into early January, with extended hours during school holidays. The atmosphere is festive rather than commercial, with the snow covered mountains providing a backdrop that no artificial decoration can match. Entry to the village is free, with charges for specific activities.

New Year's Eve celebrations combine mountain atmosphere with festive energy. Andorra la Vella hosts a public celebration in the main square, with music, countdown, and fireworks that illuminate the surrounding peaks. The ski resorts organize their own celebrations, with torchlit descents by ski instructors creating rivers of fire down the slopes. Restaurants offer special New Year menus, with reservations essential weeks in advance. The combination of celebration and snow creates a New Year experience distinct from urban equivalents. The period between Christmas and New Year is the busiest of the winter season, with accommodation at peak rates and restaurants fully booked.

The Sant Antoni festival in mid January marks the traditional start of the Andorran winter calendar. Sant Antoni, the patron saint of animals, is honored with blessings of pets and livestock, bonfires, and traditional foods. The festival is celebrated in multiple parishes, with La Massana hosting one of the largest observances. The blessing of animals draws participants with their dogs, cats, horses, and occasionally more exotic companions. The bonfires in village squares provide warmth and gathering points for communities in the depth of winter. The festival preserves traditions that predate Christianity, with the fires representing purification and the driving out of winter darkness. Visitors are welcome to observe and participate, with the bonfires providing a memorable winter experience.

Spring events March to May

Carnival arrives in February or March depending on the liturgical calendar, bringing color and celebration to the late winter weeks. The Carnival celebrations in Andorra follow the pattern of Catalan carnival traditions, with parades, costumes, and the coronation and subsequent trial of the Carnestoltes, the Carnival King. The largest celebrations take place in Andorra la Vella and Escaldes-Engordany, with parades that include floats, music, and costumed participants. The celebrations culminate on the Tuesday before Ash Wednesday, with the burial of the sardine, a mock funeral that marks the end of Carnival and the beginning of Lent. The tradition is shared with Spain and Latin America, with the Andorran version reflecting local character and humor.

Easter observances in Andorra combine the solemnity of Holy Week with the joy of Easter Sunday. The religious processions that mark Good Friday wind through village streets, with participants carrying statues and symbols of the Passion. The caramelles, traditional Easter songs, are performed by groups moving through the villages, carrying on a tradition that has persisted for centuries. The Ordino valley is particularly known for its caramelles tradition, with groups of singers maintaining repertoires passed down through generations. The songs blend religious themes with secular commentary, including verses that gently reference local events and personalities. Visitors who encounter the caramelles are witnessing a living folk tradition rather than a staged performance.

May brings the Canòlich pilgrimage in Sant Julia de Loria parish, one of the most significant religious events in the Andorran calendar. The pilgrimage honors the Virgin of Canòlich, whose statue was discovered according to legend by a shepherd in the 13th century. On the last Saturday of May, the statue is carried in procession from the parish church in Sant Julia de Loria to the sanctuary of Canòlich, a journey of several kilometers that climbs through pine forest to the hillside chapel. The pilgrimage draws participants from across Andorra, with some walking the entire route and others joining at points along the way. The event combines religious devotion with community gathering, concluding with a communal meal at the sanctuary. Visitors are welcome to walk the pilgrimage route, with the forest setting and mountain views adding natural beauty to the spiritual experience.

Summer events June to August

The summer solstice brings the falles festival, recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. On the night of June 23, participants in several Andorran villages carry flaming torches down from the mountains to light bonfires in the village squares. The tradition, shared with Pyrenean communities in Spain and France, has pre Christian origins related to the power of fire to purify and protect. The spectacle of torchlight processions descending through mountain darkness, with the fires reflecting off stone walls and illuminating the faces of participants, creates an experience of extraordinary power. The falles are celebrated in Andorra la Vella, Sant Julia de Loria, and several smaller villages. The event is a genuine community tradition, not a tourist performance, and visitors who attend are witnessing a ritual that has connected Andorrans to their ancestors for millennia.

The Andorra la Vella International Jazz Festival brings international musicians to perform in mountain settings throughout June and July. The festival has grown over the years to include established jazz artists and emerging talents, with concerts held in venues ranging from the modern auditorium to outdoor stages with mountain backdrops. The programming covers a range of jazz styles, from traditional to contemporary, with some concerts free and others ticketed. The festival atmosphere is sophisticated but relaxed, with audiences that include jazz enthusiasts and curious visitors drawn by the setting. The concerts provide cultural evenings that complement the outdoor activities of summer days.

The Ordino Classical Music Festival fills the Romanesque churches and historic venues of the Ordino valley with chamber music and orchestral performances during July and August. The acoustics of the stone churches, designed for Gregorian chant eight centuries ago, provide a setting that enhances the musical experience. The programming includes works from the classical repertoire performed by musicians from Andorra, Spain, France, and beyond. The festival draws audiences who appreciate the combination of musical quality and historic setting. Tickets are available through the festival website and at the Ordino tourist office, with advance booking recommended for popular performances.

July and August are the months of the festa major, the annual village festival celebrated in each parish on the feast day of its patron saint. These festivals combine religious observance with secular celebration, featuring masses in the parish church followed by communal meals, traditional dancing, concerts, and activities for children. Each parish celebrates on a different date, creating a rolling calendar of festivals throughout the summer. The festa major in Canillo in late July, in Andorra la Vella in early August, and in Escaldes-Engordany in late July are among the largest. The festivals are authentic community events where visitors are welcomed but expected to participate respectfully. The fireworks displays that close many festivals light up mountain valleys, the explosions echoing from the surrounding peaks.

Autumn events September to November

September 8 marks the National Day of Andorra, the feast of Our Lady of Meritxell, the country's patron saint. The day is the most significant in the Andorran civic and religious calendar. Pilgrims walk to the Sanctuary of Meritxell from across the country, some following traditional routes used for centuries. The sanctuary fills with worshippers for the mass that forms the spiritual center of the celebration. The day combines religious devotion with national pride, as Meritxell symbolizes both the Catholic heritage and the distinct identity of the Andorran people. Visitors are welcome at the sanctuary, with the atmosphere particularly moving during the pilgrimage when the building fills with families whose connection to the shrine spans generations.

September and October bring the mushroom season and its associated fairs and events. The Mushroom Fair in Escaldes-Engordany, typically held in late September or early October, celebrates the fungal bounty of the Pyrenean forests. The fair includes guided foraging walks led by experts who can distinguish the prized rovellons from their less edible lookalikes. Cooking demonstrations by local chefs show how to prepare wild mushrooms in traditional and contemporary dishes. A market sells the morning's harvest from local foragers, with the mushrooms often still carrying the scent of the pine forest floor. Restaurants throughout the country offer special mushroom menus during the season. The fair draws both serious mycologists and curious food lovers, united by appreciation for one of the mountain's most distinctive products.

October and November bring the chestnut festivals, or castanyades, that mark the autumn season across Catalan speaking territories. The festivals center on roasted chestnuts, served hot in paper cones alongside panellets, small marzipan and pine nut sweets, and sweet wine. The castanyades have pre Christian origins as harvest celebrations, overlaid with Christian associations with All Saints' Day on November 1. Villages across Andorra hold castanyades throughout October and early November, with the aroma of roasting chestnuts filling the streets. The festivals are informal community gatherings rather than organized events, with chestnut vendors setting up in squares and outside churches. Visitors who encounter a castanyada are experiencing a tradition that connects contemporary Andorrans to their agricultural past.

Religious festivals and pilgrimages

The religious calendar of Andorra reflects the country's Catholic heritage while incorporating elements that predate Christianity. The major feasts of Christmas, Easter, and the festes majors of the patron saints are observed with masses that fill the Romanesque churches, some of which have hosted the same rituals for over 800 years. The continuity of worship in these ancient spaces connects contemporary Andorrans to the generations that prayed in the same buildings, under the same frescoes, before the same altars. Visitors who attend a service in a Romanesque church, even without sharing the faith, experience the living dimension of buildings that could otherwise seem like museum pieces.

The Meritxell pilgrimage on September 8 is the most significant religious event in the Andorran year. The sanctuary, rebuilt in striking modern style after the original burned in 1972, fills with worshippers from across the country. Some walk from their home parishes, covering distances of 20 kilometers or more on foot. The mass is concelebrated by bishops and priests from Andorra and the neighboring Spanish dioceses. The statue of Our Lady of Meritxell, a replica of the Romanesque original lost in the fire, occupies the place of honor. The pilgrimage expresses Andorran national identity through religious devotion, with Meritxell serving as both patron saint and national symbol. Visitors are welcome at the sanctuary throughout the day, with the morning mass being the central event.

The Canòlich pilgrimage on the last Saturday of May connects the southern parish of Sant Julia de Loria to its spiritual center. The statue of the Virgin of Canòlich, normally housed in the parish church, is carried in procession to the hillside sanctuary where according to tradition it was discovered by a shepherd. The route climbs through pine forest, with views across the valley to the Spanish border. The pilgrimage is both a religious observance and a community gathering, with the walk providing time for conversation and the communal meal at the sanctuary strengthening parish bonds. The event is smaller and more intimate than the Meritxell pilgrimage, offering visitors a glimpse of parish level religious practice.

Music festivals and concerts

The Andorra la Vella International Jazz Festival, held in June and July, brings international jazz artists to perform in venues across the capital. The festival has grown steadily since its founding, attracting established names and emerging talents from Europe and beyond. Concerts are held in the National Auditorium, in outdoor spaces in the capital, and occasionally in more unusual venues such as Romanesque churches or mountain settings. The programming covers a range of jazz styles, with some concerts free and others ticketed. The festival adds a cultural dimension to summer visits, providing evening entertainment that complements the outdoor activities of the day.

The Ordino Classical Music Festival, running through July and August, places chamber music and orchestral performances in the historic venues of the Ordino valley. The Romanesque church of Sant Corneli i Sant Cebrià in Ordino village, with its stone walls and wooden ceiling, provides a setting of exceptional acoustic quality. Concerts are also held in other churches and historic buildings throughout the parish. The programming includes works from the classical repertoire, with an emphasis on composers connected to the Pyrenean region. The festival attracts musicians from Andorran, Spanish, and French ensembles, with occasional international soloists. The intimate scale of the venues creates a connection between performers and audience that larger concert halls cannot provide.

The International Music Festival of Andorra la Vella, held in September, extends the summer music season into early autumn. The festival programs classical and contemporary music in venues across the capital, including the National Auditorium and the historic Casa de la Vall. The festival often includes choral music, with the acoustics of the Romanesque churches particularly suited to vocal performance. The autumn timing allows the festival to draw performers who are touring Europe as the concert season begins, bringing international quality to Andorran venues. The festival is smaller than its Ordino counterpart but offers high quality performances in accessible settings.

Sporting events and competitions

The UCI Mountain Bike World Cup has held rounds at the Vallnord Bike Park in La Massana, bringing the world's best downhill and cross country riders to compete on Andorran trails. The event typically takes place in July or August, drawing spectators who line the courses to watch riders tackle the technical terrain at competition speed. The atmosphere is festive, with the mountain setting providing a dramatic backdrop to the athletic competition. Even when the World Cup is not scheduled, Vallnord hosts national and regional competitions throughout the summer, offering opportunities to watch competitive mountain biking in a spectacular setting. The bike park trails are open to the public when not being used for competition, allowing visitors to ride the same terrain they have watched the professionals tackle.

The Andorra Ultra Trail, held in July, challenges runners on courses that traverse the principality's most dramatic mountain terrain. The event includes multiple distance options, from a 10 kilometer introductory trail run to the 170 kilometer Ronda dels Cims that circles the entire country. The Ultra Trail draws participants from across Europe, with the extreme elevation changes and technical terrain providing a test that few other races match. Spectators can watch the start and finish in Ordino, with the atmosphere combining athletic intensity with mountain festival spirit. The event demonstrates Andorra's capacity to host world class sporting competitions that use the natural terrain as the primary infrastructure.

The Tour de France and Vuelta a España have both included Andorran stages over the years, bringing the world's most famous cycling races to Pyrenean roads. When a Grand Tour visits Andorra, the mountain roads fill with spectators who camp for days to secure prime viewing positions on the climbs. The atmosphere is electric, with the caravan of support vehicles and sponsors passing hours before the riders, building anticipation for the moment when the race arrives. Even when the Grand Tours are not visiting, Andorra's roads are popular with amateur cyclists who come to test themselves on the same climbs. Cycling events are announced well in advance, allowing visitors to plan trips around race dates.

Food and wine festivals

The Mushroom Fair in Escaldes-Engordany, typically held in late September or early October, celebrates the wild mushrooms that fruit in Pyrenean forests during the autumn rains. The fair includes a market where local foragers sell the morning's harvest, with varieties ranging from the prized ceps to the delicate chanterelles. Expert led foraging walks teach participants to identify edible species and avoid toxic lookalikes. Cooking demonstrations show how to prepare mushrooms in traditional dishes and contemporary cuisine. Restaurants throughout the country offer special mushroom menus during the fair period. The event draws food lovers, mycologists, and anyone who appreciates the connection between the mountain landscape and the food it produces.

The calçotada season, running from January through April, is less a festival than a seasonal dining tradition that becomes a social event. Calçots, large spring onions grilled over open fires until charred outside and tender within, are eaten with romesco sauce in communal feasts. Restaurants and community groups organize calçotades throughout the season, with participants gathered around long tables wearing bibs to protect their clothes. The tradition originated in Catalonia and has been enthusiastically adopted by Andorrans. The calçotada is informal and convivial, with the messy process of peeling charred onions and dipping them in sauce breaking down formal barriers between diners. Visitors who participate in a calçotada experience Andorran food culture at its most social and least pretentious.

The chestnut festivals, or castanyades, in October and November celebrate the autumn chestnut harvest with roasted chestnuts, panellets, and sweet wine. The festivals are informal community gatherings rather than ticketed events, with chestnut vendors appearing in village squares and outside churches. The aroma of roasting chestnuts signals the arrival of autumn and the turning of the year. The castanyades preserve connections to the agricultural past, when chestnuts were a vital food source for mountain communities. The tradition is shared across Catalan speaking territories, with the Andorran version reflecting local character and the mountain setting.

UNESCO intangible heritage events

The falles festival, recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2015, takes place on the summer solstice in several Andorran villages. The tradition involves carrying flaming torches down from the mountains to light bonfires in village squares, a ritual with origins in pre Christian fire worship and purification ceremonies. The UNESCO recognition acknowledges the cultural significance of the tradition and the communities that maintain it. The falles are celebrated in Andorra la Vella, Sant Julia de Loria, Encamp, and several smaller villages. Each community has its own variations, but the core elements, the torches, the descent from the mountain, and the communal bonfire, are shared. Visitors who witness the falles are experiencing a tradition that connects contemporary Andorrans to their deepest cultural roots.

The transhumance, the seasonal movement of livestock between lowland winter pastures and high mountain summer grazing, continues in Andorra as it has for centuries. While not formally listed as UNESCO heritage, the practice is recognized as part of the cultural landscape that earned the Madriu Valley its World Heritage status. The movement of sheep and cattle to high pastures in June and their return in September are marked by community events in some villages, with the animals passing through village streets on their way to and from the mountains. The transhumance represents the agricultural foundation on which Andorran culture was built, and its continuation maintains a living link to that past.

Village festa major calendar

Each Andorran parish celebrates its festa major on the feast day of its patron saint. The festivals follow a pattern established over centuries: mass in the parish church, communal meals, traditional dancing including the sardana and contrapàs, concerts, activities for children, and fireworks. The festivals are authentic community events, with parishioners gathering to renew bonds and transmit traditions. Visitors are welcomed warmly but expected to participate respectfully. The major festa major dates are spread through the summer and early autumn:

Canillo celebrates in late July, honoring Sant Joan de Caselles with services in the Romanesque church and celebrations in the village. Escaldes-Engordany celebrates in late July, with the thermal waters that define the parish incorporated into the festivities. Andorra la Vella celebrates in early August, with the largest urban festival including concerts, markets, and fireworks. La Massana celebrates in mid August, with outdoor events taking advantage of the mountain setting. Ordino celebrates in mid September, with the most traditional atmosphere of the major festivals. Encamp celebrates in late August, combining religious observance with mountain sports events. Sant Julia de Loria celebrates in late July, with the agricultural heritage of the southern parish reflected in the festivities.

Planning around events

Planning a trip around an Andorran event requires advance research and flexibility. The exact dates of some events, particularly those tied to the liturgical calendar like Easter and Carnival, vary from year to year. The tourist office websites publish updated event calendars annually, with the summer festival dates typically confirmed by early spring. Accommodation during major events, particularly the festes majors and the Christmas period, books out well in advance. Restaurants fill for festival dinners, and the most popular events may require tickets purchased ahead of time.

The benefits of timing a visit to coincide with an event are substantial. A festival provides access to experiences that no amount of money can buy at other times: the falles torches descending through darkness, the sardana danced in a village square, the fireworks echoing from mountain walls. The atmosphere of a village during its festa major is entirely different from its atmosphere on an ordinary day. The events provide windows into Andorran culture that remain closed during ordinary times. The crowds that accompany major events are the price of admission to these experiences, but crowds in Andorra remain manageable compared to festival crowds in larger destinations.

For visitors who prefer quiet to celebration, avoiding major events is equally valid. The villages during their festes majors are noisy and crowded by Andorran standards. The Christmas markets draw day trippers from Spain and France in numbers that can overwhelm the capital's infrastructure. The shoulder months of May and October offer the quietest experience, with few events and low visitor numbers. The choice between event focused and quiet travel depends entirely on preference, with Andorra offering both experiences in different seasons.

Event etiquette for visitors

Participating in Andorran festivals as a visitor requires awareness of the difference between public events and community celebrations. The festa major is not a tourist performance but a community gathering that visitors are permitted to attend. Respecting the religious dimension of festivals, even without sharing the faith, means maintaining quiet during masses and processions. Respecting the social dimension means not inserting yourself into communal meals that are intended for parishioners unless specifically invited. The appropriate stance is appreciative observation with willingness to participate when welcomed.

Photography at festivals requires sensitivity. The falles, with their dramatic torchlight and fire, are irresistibly photogenic, but the participants are engaged in a meaningful ritual, not performing for cameras. Using flash during nighttime events is intrusive and may be prohibited. Asking permission before photographing individuals, particularly during religious observances, shows respect for the people whose culture you are documenting. The best festival photographs often come from a position of patient observation rather than aggressive pursuit of images.

Practical festival etiquette includes arriving early for popular events, dressing appropriately for religious services, and following local customs regarding food and drink. The Spanish and Andorran custom of late dining applies during festivals, with communal meals often starting at 9 PM or later. Adapting to local rhythms rather than expecting accommodation to visitor schedules enriches the experience. The effort made to understand and respect local customs is usually appreciated and reciprocated with warmer welcome.

The events of Andorra are not decorations on the tourist calendar but expressions of a living culture. The flames of the falles, the voices of the caramelles, the footsteps of pilgrims on the road to Meritxell, and the music echoing from Romanesque stone are not performances. They are the continuation of traditions that have defined these mountain valleys for centuries. Visitors who arrange their travels to witness these events gain more than entertainment. They gain insight into what it means to be Andorran, to carry forward a heritage that the modern world has not erased. The principality's festivals remind us that tradition is not the opposite of progress but its companion, that the past can be honored without being frozen, and that communities that celebrate together remain communities. The calendar invites you to be present, to witness, and perhaps to understand something about this small country that no guidebook can fully convey.
