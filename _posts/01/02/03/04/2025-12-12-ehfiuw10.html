A trip to Andorra can be whatever you want it to be. A long weekend of skiing with friends. A week of summer hiking with the family. A solo journey through stone villages and mountain refuges. A couples escape built around spa visits and long dinners in rustic bordas. The principality packs enough variety into its 468 square kilometers to fill itineraries ranging from two days to two weeks. What makes Andorra different from larger destinations is the absence of wasted time. You are never more than 45 minutes from your next activity. A morning on the slopes can flow into an afternoon at the spa and an evening in a medieval village without hours of driving in between. This guide provides practical itineraries, budget frameworks, packing recommendations, and planning strategies that help you build a trip matching your available time, travel style, and interests.

Planning your Andorra trip

The first decision in planning an Andorra trip is the season. Winter means skiing, snowboarding, snowshoeing, thermal spas, and duty free shopping. Summer means hiking, mountain biking, via ferrata, canyoning, and village exploration. Spring and autumn shoulder seasons offer lower prices, fewer visitors, and a mix of activities depending on snow conditions and weather. The choice of season dictates everything from your packing list to your base location to your daily rhythm. There is no wrong answer, only the right answer for the experience you want.

The second decision is your base. The capital, Andorra la Vella, provides the most accommodation options, restaurants, and shopping. La Massana and Ordino offer quieter bases closer to hiking and Vallnord skiing. Canillo, El Tarter, and Soldeu put you in the heart of the Grandvalira ski area. Pas de la Casa suits budget skiers and nightlife seekers. Each base shapes the trip differently. A ski trip based in the capital requires a daily commute to the slopes. A hiking trip based in Soldeu means longer drives to western trailheads. Matching your base to your primary activity minimizes travel time and maximizes enjoyment.

The third decision involves transportation. A rental car provides maximum flexibility, particularly for hikers who want to access remote trailheads and explore multiple valleys. Public transport works well for skiers based in resort villages and for visitors who plan to stay primarily in one area. The bus network connects all major towns and ski areas efficiently and affordably. The trade off is between the freedom of a car and the simplicity of public transport. Many visitors split the difference, using buses for airport transfers and renting a car only for specific days when exploration plans demand it.

Best time to visit by activity

Skiing and snowboarding dominate from December through April, with the most reliable snow conditions in January and February. The Christmas and New Year period brings festive atmosphere and peak prices. February offers excellent snow and longer days, with the half term holiday week bringing families from across Europe. March delivers a sweet spot of softer snow, warmer temperatures, and reduced crowds after the February rush. Early April still offers good skiing at higher elevations, with the bonus of skiing in a t-shirt on sunny afternoons. The ski season varies slightly each year based on snowfall, but the extensive snowmaking coverage ensures consistent conditions on main runs.

Hiking season runs from late May through October, with the optimal window in July and August when all trails are snow free. Late June brings peak wildflower displays in the high meadows. September offers golden light, cooler temperatures, and empty trails as summer crowds depart. October adds autumn colors, with the larch forests turning brilliant shades of orange and gold. Lower elevation trails open in May and stay accessible into November. The high mountain passes typically clear by late June and may see first snow in late September. Hikers should check current conditions with tourist offices before setting out on high routes in shoulder months.

Shopping and wellness operate year round, with the best deals on accommodation found in May and October. The thermal spas are particularly appealing in winter when the contrast between cold air and warm water feels most dramatic. Summer shopping benefits from sales periods in July. The cultural calendar peaks in summer with music festivals and village celebrations. Winter brings Christmas markets and festive atmosphere to the capital and larger towns. There is no month when Andorra feels closed or unwelcoming, only months when specific activities are at their best.

Andorra in 2 days

A two day visit works as a weekend escape or an add on to a longer Pyrenees road trip. The key is accepting that you cannot see everything and focusing on a single valley or theme. The most satisfying two day itinerary combines the capital's urban energy with a taste of the mountain landscapes that define the country.

Day one: Arrive in Andorra la Vella by mid morning. Park your car or drop your bags at your hotel and walk the length of Avinguda Meritxell, the main shopping artery. Even if you are not a serious shopper, the street gives you a sense of the capital's commercial energy. Stop for coffee at a terrace cafe and watch the flow of visitors from Spain, France, and beyond. Visit the church of Santa Coloma on the edge of the capital, taking in the circular bell tower that dates from the 9th century. Lunch at a restaurant in the old quarter, trying escudella if the weather is cool or grilled meat if the sun is shining. Spend the afternoon at Caldea thermal spa, soaking in the lagoon and moving between the hydrotherapy pools. As evening falls, enjoy the outdoor pool with mountain views. Dinner at a borda in Escaldes-Engordany or a short drive up to La Massana for a more rustic experience. Try trinxat, the cabbage and potato cake that defines Andorran mountain food.

Day two: Rise early and drive to the Ordino valley. Spend the morning walking through Ordino village, the prettiest in Andorra, with its stone houses, slate roofs, and flower decked balconies. Visit the manor house museum for a dose of Andorran history. Continue up the valley to La Cortinada to see the Romanesque church of Sant Martí with its original frescoes. If energy and weather permit, drive further to the Ordino-Arcalis ski area and walk the short trail to the Tristaina Lakes. The hike takes about three hours round trip and rewards with three glacial lakes in a high cirque. Picnic beside the water before descending. Late afternoon return to the capital for any last shopping or a final coffee before departure. If flying from Barcelona, allow three hours for the drive. From Toulouse, allow two hours and forty five minutes.

This two day itinerary works best with a rental car, as it involves moving between the capital and the Ordino valley. Public transport can cover the same ground with more time spent waiting for buses. The itinerary is intentionally full but not rushed, with each activity given enough time to be enjoyed rather than checked off a list. The memory you take away will be of mountain beauty and urban comfort existing side by side, which is exactly the Andorran experience.

Andorra in 3 days

Three days opens up the possibility of experiencing two distinct valleys while still keeping the capital as your cultural and culinary anchor. This itinerary works for summer visitors and can be adapted for winter by swapping hiking for skiing or snowshoeing.

Day one: Follow the day one plan from the two day itinerary above, covering the capital, Santa Coloma, and Caldea. The first day acclimates you to Andorra and provides the urban context for the mountain days that follow.

Day two: Dedicate the full day to the Ordino valley and high mountain hiking. Start with the Tristaina Lakes circuit in the morning when the light is best for photography and the trails are quietest. After the hike, drive down to Ordino village for a late lunch at a borda. Spend the afternoon exploring the village and visiting the miniature museum or simply sitting in the square with a drink. Drive up to the Mirador del Roc del Quer for panoramic views across the central valleys as the afternoon light turns golden. Return to your base for dinner.

Day three: Head east to the Canillo area for a different mountain experience. Visit Sant Joan de Caselles, the finest Romanesque church in Andorra, standing alone in a meadow with mountains behind. Continue to the Incles Valley and walk the flat track along the valley floor, past grazing horses and stone barns. The walk takes about two hours round trip at a gentle pace. For a more challenging option, continue up to the Juclar refuge for lunch before descending. Afternoon visit to the Sanctuary of Meritxell, the national shrine rebuilt in striking modern style after the original burned. The contrast between the ancient Romanesque of Sant Joan and the contemporary architecture of Meritxell tells the story of Andorra's relationship with its heritage. Return to the capital for a farewell dinner.

Andorra in 5 days

Five days allows you to explore all three major valleys, add an adventure activity, and still have time for relaxation. This itinerary balances activity with recovery and urban with rural, creating a rhythm that leaves you refreshed rather than exhausted.

Day one: Arrival and orientation in Andorra la Vella. Afternoon walk through the capital, visit Santa Coloma church, evening at Caldea thermal spa. Dinner in the old quarter.

Day two: Ordino valley exploration. Morning hike to the Tristaina Lakes or the Sorteny Natural Park depending on fitness and interests. Lunch in Ordino village. Afternoon visit to La Cortinada church and the restored flour mill. Evening at a borda in Llorts or back in Ordino.

Day three: Adventure day. Choose your activity based on season and preference: via ferrata at Canal de la Mora, canyoning in the southern canyons, or mountain biking at Vallnord Bike Park. Book a guided experience through a local operator. The activity will take most of the morning and early afternoon. Late afternoon recovery at your hotel or a return visit to Caldea. Casual dinner in La Massana or the capital.

Day four: Eastern valleys day. Morning at Sant Joan de Caselles and the Incles Valley. Lunch in Canillo. Afternoon hike in the Pessons cirque, accessed by the summer lift from Grau Roig. The high altitude lake landscape provides a dramatic finale to your mountain exploration. Evening farewell dinner at a special restaurant, perhaps the Sport Hotel Hermitage in Soldeu or a celebrated borda in Ordino.

Day five: Leisurely morning with final shopping or a short walk. Late breakfast at a cafe. Depart for your airport, allowing time for the drive and any border formalities.

Andorra in 7 days

A full week in Andorra allows you to slow down, explore deeply, and include a full day dedicated to rest and wellness. This itinerary works for summer visitors who want to hike extensively while also enjoying the cultural and culinary offerings.

Day one: Arrival and settlement. Afternoon orientation walk in the capital. Evening at Caldea. Welcome dinner at a restaurant in the old quarter.

Day two: Ordino valley day. Morning hike in Sorteny Natural Park, one of the richest botanical reserves in the Pyrenees. Lunch at the Sorteny refuge or back in Ordino village. Afternoon exploring Ordino's streets and shops. Dinner at a borda.

Day three: High mountain day. Drive to the Comapedrosa trailhead for the challenging hike to Andorra's highest summit. This is a full day commitment requiring an early start and good fitness. Alternative: hike the Tristaina Lakes circuit or the Pessons cirque for a less demanding high mountain experience. Pack lunch and plenty of water. Evening recovery at your hotel.

Day four: Wellness and culture day. Sleep in and enjoy a late breakfast. Morning visit to the Carmen Thyssen Museum in Escaldes-Engordany. Lunch at a restaurant with a terrace. Afternoon spa session at Caldea Inuu, the adults only premium area. The contrast between morning culture and afternoon thermal bathing creates a deeply restorative day. Dinner at a restaurant you have been wanting to try.

Day five: Eastern valleys day. Morning at Sant Joan de Caselles and the Incles Valley walk. Lunch in Canillo. Afternoon adventure activity: via ferrata, canyoning, or mountain biking depending on interest. Evening in Soldeu or El Tarter for a change of scene.

Day six: Hidden villages day. Explore the smaller settlements that most visitors miss. Drive to Certers and Fontaneda in the southern parish of Sant Julia de Loria. Walk through Aixirivall and visit its Romanesque church. Afternoon drive to the Sanctuary of Canòlich for views and history. Final evening dinner at a special restaurant.

Day seven: Departure day. Morning walk through the capital for any last purchases or photographs. Leisurely breakfast. Depart for the airport with time for the drive and any stops along the way.

Ski trip itineraries

A ski trip itinerary differs fundamentally from a summer trip, with the slopes dictating the daily rhythm. The key decision is which resort to focus on and where to base yourself. Grandvalira offers the largest domain with 210 kilometers of pistes, while Vallnord provides a more intimate experience with excellent off piste at Ordino-Arcalis.

Five day ski trip based in Soldeu or El Tarter: Spend four full days skiing Grandvalira, exploring different sectors each day. Day one: Soldeu and El Tarter to orient yourself and warm up the legs. Day two: head to Grau Roig and Pas de la Casa for higher altitude terrain and more challenging runs. Day three: return to your favorite sectors and work on technique with a morning lesson. Day four: explore Canillo and Encamp sectors, with a long lunch at a mountain restaurant. Evenings: apres ski in Soldeu, dinner at hotel or village restaurants, one night at Caldea for muscle recovery.

Five day ski trip based in La Massana: Spend three days skiing Vallnord Pal-Arinsal, focusing on technique and enjoying the quieter slopes. Day trip to Ordino-Arcalis for off piste and freeride terrain if conditions permit. One day trip to Grandvalira for scale and variety, using the bus connection or driving. Evenings: relaxed apres in La Massana, dinner at local restaurants, spa evening at Caldea.

Ski trip planning requires advance booking for peak periods. Lift passes, equipment rental, and ski school places should be reserved before arrival, particularly for Christmas, New Year, and February half term. The savings from early booking are significant, and availability cannot be guaranteed for walk up purchases during the busiest weeks.

Summer adventure itineraries

Summer adventure trips center on the activities that define Andorran warm weather travel. The itineraries below assume good fitness and a willingness to try multiple activities. They can be adapted by swapping activities or adding rest days.

Four day adventure trip: Day one: via ferrata at Canal de la Mora in the morning, followed by lunch in Canillo and an afternoon walk in the Incles Valley. Day two: full day canyoning in the Ordino canyons, with the evening at Caldea for muscle recovery. Day three: mountain biking at Vallnord Bike Park, starting with green and blue trails before progressing to reds if skills allow. Day four: morning hike to the Tristaina Lakes or the Comapedrosa summit depending on fitness, followed by a farewell lunch and departure.

Week long adventure trip: Spread the activities across more days with rest periods in between. Add rock climbing at Sant Antoni, a second biking day exploring cross country trails, and a guided off piste skiing or snowboarding day if visiting in winter. Include a full rest day with spa and cultural activities. The week format allows you to try activities that require instruction and progression, such as learning to mountain bike downhill or completing an advanced via ferrata route.

Family trip itineraries

Family trips require pacing that accommodates shorter attention spans, earlier bedtimes, and the need for downtime between activities. Andorra's compact geography helps, as activities are never far from your base and the option to retreat to the hotel for a rest is always available.

Five day family summer trip: Day one: arrival and gentle orientation. Afternoon walk in the capital, visit to a playground, early dinner. Day two: Naturlandia adventure park for the Tobotronc alpine coaster, high rope courses, and animal park. Full day with picnic lunch. Day three: morning hike to the Tristaina Lakes at a child friendly pace, with plenty of stops. Afternoon rest at the hotel. Evening pizza dinner. Day four: morning at the Sorteny Natural Park visitor center with its interactive exhibits. Afternoon pony riding or gentle horseback excursion. Day five: morning at Caldea family session in the thermal lagoon. Departure after lunch.

Five day family winter trip: Day one: arrival and equipment rental. Afternoon settling in. Day two: ski school morning for children and adults. Afternoon practice on gentle slopes. Day three: ski school continuation. Afternoon family skiing on green runs. Day four: morning skiing, afternoon snowshoe walk or tobogganing at Naturlandia. Day five: morning at Caldea family session. Departure after lunch. The key to family ski trips is limiting ski time to what children can enjoy and building in non ski activities that keep everyone happy.

Romantic couples itineraries

Andorra suits couples seeking a mix of activity, relaxation, and romantic settings. The mountain bordas with their open fires, the thermal spas with evening sessions under the stars, and the quiet stone villages create an atmosphere that encourages connection.

Three day couples escape: Day one: arrive and settle into a boutique hotel in Ordino or a spa hotel in Escaldes-Engordany. Afternoon walk through Ordino village or the capital's old quarter. Evening at Caldea Inuu, the adults only wellness area, followed by dinner at a candlelit borda. Day two: morning hike to the Tristaina Lakes, taking your time and enjoying the mountain solitude. Picnic beside the water. Afternoon couples massage at your hotel spa. Evening at a special restaurant, perhaps the Sport Hotel Hermitage or a celebrated borda. Day three: leisurely morning with breakfast on a terrace. Drive to the Mirador del Roc del Quer for final mountain views. Depart after lunch.

Five day couples retreat: Extend the three day itinerary with a day of adventure activity shared together, via ferrata or canyoning, and a day exploring the eastern valleys including Sant Joan de Caselles, the Incles Valley, and the Sanctuary of Meritxell. Add a second spa session and a cooking class or wine tasting if available through your hotel. The extended time allows the relaxed pace that makes a couples trip feel like a genuine escape from routine.

Andorra budget breakdown

Andorra offers value compared to equivalent Alpine destinations, but costs can vary significantly based on season, accommodation choices, and activities. The figures below represent approximate ranges per person per day, excluding international flights.

Budget travel: 60 to 90 euros per day. This covers a bed in a budget hotel or hostel, self catered meals supplemented with menú del dia lunches, public transport, and one paid activity every other day. Shopping and spa visits are minimal at this budget level. The budget works best for summer hikers who spend their days on free trails and their evenings in simple accommodations. Pas de la Casa offers budget ski options with shared apartments and self catering.

Mid range travel: 120 to 180 euros per day. This covers a comfortable three star hotel or apartment, restaurant meals including a mix of menú del dia and à la carte dinners, a rental car or regular taxi use, and daily activities such as lift passes, equipment rental, or guided excursions. This is the sweet spot for most Andorra visitors, providing comfort and flexibility without extravagance. Ski trips at this budget require advance booking for the best lift pass and rental rates.

Luxury travel: 250 to 400 plus euros per day. This covers four or five star hotels with spa facilities, fine dining restaurants, private transfers or premium rental cars, private guided activities, and regular spa treatments. Soldeu and Grau Roig offer the most luxury ski experiences. Ordino and Escaldes-Engordany provide luxury summer bases. The value at this level comes from the quality of the experience relative to luxury offerings in more famous destinations.

What to pack for Andorra

Packing for Andorra requires preparation for mountain weather regardless of season. The altitude means temperatures can change rapidly, and a sunny morning can turn into a rainy or snowy afternoon. Layers provide the flexibility to adapt to changing conditions.

Winter packing essentials: Waterproof ski jacket and pants, thermal base layers, mid layer fleece or insulated jacket, ski socks, gloves or mittens, hat or helmet compatible beanie, neck gaiter or buff, sunscreen and lip balm with high SPF, sunglasses and goggles. For non ski time: warm boots with good grip, casual clothes for restaurants and bars, swimwear for the spa. The altitude intensifies sun exposure, so sun protection is essential even on cloudy days.

Summer packing essentials: Hiking boots with ankle support, waterproof jacket and trousers, breathable hiking shirts and pants, warm fleece or lightweight insulated jacket for high altitudes, hat for sun protection, sunglasses, sunscreen, insect repellent for lower elevations. For non hiking time: casual clothes for towns and restaurants, swimwear for spa and lake swimming. A headlamp is useful for early starts and emergency situations. Trekking poles reduce knee strain on descents.

Year round essentials: Travel adapter for European two pin plugs, portable power bank for phone charging on long trail days, reusable water bottle, basic first aid kit including blister treatment, any personal medications, and travel insurance documents. Cash in euros is useful for mountain refuges and smaller establishments that may not accept cards.

Practical travel tips

The practical details of traveling in Andorra are straightforward once you understand a few key points. The official language is Catalan, but Spanish and French are universally understood, and English is common in tourist areas. The euro is the currency despite Andorra not being an EU member. Credit cards work in most establishments, though smaller refuges and rural restaurants may be cash only.

Mobile phone coverage is generally good, but Andorra is outside the EU roaming zone. Check your mobile plan before traveling to avoid unexpected charges. Many visitors purchase a local prepaid SIM for data during longer stays. Wi-Fi is standard in hotels and cafes. Tap water is safe to drink, and carrying a refillable bottle reduces plastic waste and saves money.

Travel insurance is essential, particularly for visitors planning adventure activities or skiing. Standard policies may exclude off piste skiing, via ferrata, canyoning, and hiking above certain elevations. Check the fine print and consider a policy designed for adventure sports. The Andorran healthcare system is excellent, but treatment without insurance can be expensive. Mountain rescue services charge for callouts, a cost that appropriate insurance covers.

Booking and resources

Advance booking is recommended for peak season travel, particularly Christmas, New Year, February half term, and August. Accommodation, lift passes, equipment rental, and popular restaurants should be reserved weeks or months ahead for these periods. Outside peak times, booking a few days to a week ahead is usually sufficient, with last minute availability common in shoulder seasons.

Official tourist office websites provide current information on trail conditions, events, and practical matters. The Andorra Tourism website covers the entire country, while individual parish tourist offices offer local detail. Booking platforms cover most Andorran accommodation, though some smaller guesthouses and rural properties book directly. Contacting properties by email or phone often yields better rates and more flexible terms than third party platforms.

Activity providers, including ski schools, via ferrata guides, and canyoning operators, generally accept online bookings. Booking directly with the provider ensures clear communication about requirements and conditions. For winter activities, the Grandvalira and Vallnord websites handle lift passes, equipment rental, and ski school bookings. Summer activities are more fragmented across providers, with tourist offices serving as useful central points of contact.

Andorra does not demand that you see everything. The country is small enough that you could, in theory, drive every road and visit every village in a week.
